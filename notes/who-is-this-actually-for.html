On Sharing Research Effectively

A decision framework for sharing research when your goal is impact, not prestige

There is a version of this question that admittedly does sounds like laziness: do I really need to write the full paper?

But given how the nature of being able to ask questions, discover, and share answers is changing so fundamentally, there is a more honest version that sounds more appropriate: what is the right amount of formalisation for this particular piece of work, given who needs to use it, and when?

Traditionally, the academic publishing pipeline was built for a world where knowledge was scarce, access was controlled, and institutional validation was the only credible signal of quality. That world has changed. A 2025 paper in PNAS formally documented what many researchers already felt: academic publishing serves two goals that are structurally misaligned: disseminating knowledge and establishing credentials. And the system has quietly optimised for the latter at the expense of the former.

Moreover, publication cycles measured in years. Most published work read by almost no one outside a narrow academic audience. Practitioners, policymakers, and builders (the people most likely to act on findings) are largely unreachable through journals. Studies suggest practitioners engage with less than 15% of published academic work. So the pipeline designed to produce impact often doesn't.

Meanwhile, preprint servers like arXiv and SSRN now function as real-time dissemination channels in fast-moving fields; roughly 77% of computer science preprints eventually reach peer-reviewed publication, meaning the two are complementary rather than competing. Academics are among the fastest-growing categories on Substack. A 2023 Knowledge Exchange study identified 45 platforms now operating as part of the alternative publishing ecosystem, not to mention cultivating a new readership (see personal favourite Prof J Fourie's Our Long Walk if you haven't already).

Don't get me wrong. None of this means peer review is useless. Far from it. For high-stakes claims, the credibility stack surely still matters, and shortcuts carry real costs. The problem is not that formal publication exists. The problem is treating it as the default for everything, regardless of what the work is for.

But it's worth asking before sharing, who actually needs this, what do they need to do with it, and how much would it cost them if I'm wrong?

Those three variables — audience, purpose, and stakes — determine most of what follows: how much methodology to show, how fast to move, which channels to use, and what kind of credibility-building to do in place of formal peer review. Transparency about methods, explicit acknowledgment of limitations, open data where possible, and genuine openness to critique can carry significant weight — not as replacements for rigour, but as visible evidence of it.

Resources

Trueblood, J. S., Allison, D. B., Field, S. M., Fishbach, A., et al. (2025). The misalignment of incentives in academic publishing and implications for journal reform. PNAS, 122(5), e2401231121. The formal academic case for the argument this post makes informally — that dissemination and credentialling are structurally in tension, and the system has optimised for the wrong one.

Fraser, N., Brierley, L., Dey, G., Polka, J. K., Pálfy, M., Nanni, F., & Coates, J. A. (2021). The evolving role of preprints in the dissemination of COVID-19 research and their impact on the science communication landscape. PLOS Biology, 19(4), e3000959. Documents how the pandemic accelerated the legitimisation of preprints as real-time communication tools — a shift that has not reversed.

Lutz, J-F., Sondervan, J., van Edig, X., Freeman, A., Kramer, B., & Rosenkrantz Hansen, C. (2023). Knowledge Exchange Analysis Report on Alternative Publishing Platforms. The most comprehensive survey of the alternative publishing ecosystem; identified 45 platforms operating outside traditional journal infrastructure.

Mastroianni, A. (2022, December 13). The rise and fall of peer review. Experimental History (Substack). A sharp, evidence-led argument that peer review functions less well as a quality filter than its institutional prestige implies — and what might replace it.

Buranyi, S. (2017, June 27). Is the staggeringly profitable business of scientific publishing bad for science? The Guardian. A useful orienting piece on how the commercial structure of academic publishing created the incentive misalignments researchers now routinely complain about.
